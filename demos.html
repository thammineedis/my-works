These steps outline a commonly used design process known as the "Design Thinking" process, which is a human-centered approach to problem-solving. Let's break down each step: (A). EMPATHISE This step involves understanding the needs, wants, and challenges of the people you are designing for. It's about putting yourself in the shoes of the user and gaining deep empathy for their experiences. This often involves activities such as interviews, observations, and immersing yourself in the user's environment to gather insights. (B). DEFINE In this step, you analyze and synthesize the information gathered during the EMPATHISE phase to define the core problems and challenges faced by the users. It's about reframing the problem in a human-centered way, focusing on the needs and aspirations of the users rather than jumping to solutions. (C). IDEATE Once you have a clear understanding of the problem, it's time to generate as many creative solutions as possible. This phase encourages brainstorming and thinking outside the box. The goal is to come up with a wide range of ideas without judgment, fostering creativity and innovation. (D). PROTOTYPE In this step, you start turning your ideas into tangible representations that can be shared and tested with others. Prototypes can take various forms, from simple sketches or mock-ups to more advanced prototypes depending on the complexity of the solution. The key is to create something quickly and cheaply to gather feedback and iterate. (E). TESTING Here, you gather feedback from users by testing your prototypes. This step is crucial for validating your ideas and ensuring that they meet the needs of the users. Testing allows you to identify strengths and weaknesses in your design and make improvements before moving forward. (F). IMPLEMENTATION Once you've refined your solution based on feedback from testing, it's time to bring it to life. Implementation involves scaling up your solution and integrating it into the real world. This may involve collaboration with stakeholders, refining the design based on further feedback, and planning for long-term sustainability. 4.
